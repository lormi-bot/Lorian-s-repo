# Meeting Closer

Close the meeting with a concrete decision, committed actions, and a warmer relationship than when it started.

> **v1 — Baseline.** Update after real meetings with close language that landed well.

## The Purpose

The close is where meetings succeed or fail to create value. A good presentation with a vague close produces nothing. A tense meeting with a warm, specific close can still produce a committed decision and a repaired relationship.

The close has two jobs:
1. **Make the decision concrete** — so everyone leaves knowing exactly what was decided
2. **Make people feel good about the meeting** — so they want to come back and partner with Lorian again

## Two Modes

**Preparation mode** — before the meeting: coach on close language, prepare the action structure, decide on relationship acknowledgment moves

**Post-meeting mode** — after the meeting: draft the follow-up summary based on what was actually decided

## Close Language by Outcome Type

### Full Decision Made

The exec team has committed to the recommendation (or a variant of it).

**In-room close:**
> "Let me summarise what we've agreed — [restate the decision in one sentence]. The next steps are [actions with owners and dates]. I'll send a written summary by [tomorrow/end of day]. Does that capture it correctly?"

**Key move:** Ask the confirmation question explicitly — "Does that capture it correctly?" — so the decision is affirmed out loud, not just implied.

**Relationship move:** Before leaving, acknowledge a specific contribution:
> "I want to say — [exec name]'s point about [X] actually made the recommendation stronger. I'll incorporate that."

This costs nothing and builds significant goodwill. It also makes Lorian memorable as someone who listens, not just presents.

### Partial Decision Made

The exec team has committed to part of the recommendation; one or more elements remain open.

**In-room close:**
> "We've made a clear decision on [part A]. The remaining question is [specific question — not 'we need more analysis']. I'll [specific action] by [date] and bring it back at [forum]. Is everyone aligned on [part A] proceeding while I work on [part B]?"

**Key move:** Name the partial commitment explicitly so it doesn't dissolve between the meeting and the follow-up. "Aligned on [part A]" is a commitment; "we'll see" is not.

### Deferral

The exec team has not committed to anything today.

**First move — surface the specific blocker:**
> "Before we close — can we name the specific question that needs to be resolved before we can move forward? I want to make sure I'm working on the right thing."

**If the blocker surfaces:**
> "That's useful. I'll have an answer to that by [date]. Can we agree to revisit at [specific forum]? I'll send a summary of today and a specific proposal for that session."

**If the blocker is vague:**
> "I hear that the timing isn't right. Can we agree on a specific date to revisit — so we don't lose the momentum from today's conversation?"

**What to avoid:** Accepting "let's follow up" without a specific date, forum, and question. "Let's follow up" is a polite no.

**Graceful close when full deferral is unavoidable:**
> "I appreciate the input — let me take that away and come back with [specific thing] by [date]. I'm glad we had the conversation — it's made the thinking sharper."

## Action Commitment Structure

Every action from the meeting needs six elements:

| Element | Description | Example |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| **Action** | Specific, unambiguous — what gets done | "Produce revised pipeline analysis with updated assumptions" |
| **Responsible owner** | The person who does the work | Lorian |
| **Accountable exec** | The exec who owns the outcome | CRO |
| **Deadline** | Specific date | 15 March |
| **Monitoring** | How progress is tracked and by whom | Weekly pipeline review; Lorian reports to CRO |
| **Escalation path** | What happens if the action stalls | CRO escalates to CEO if not resolved by 22 March |

**The RACI principle:** Responsibility (who does it) and accountability (who owns the outcome) must be named separately. Diffuse accountability — "the team" or "we" — is how actions disappear.

**Monitoring cadence patterns:**
- High-urgency action (Q4 deadline) → weekly check-in, named forum
- Medium-urgency (30-60 day) → bi-weekly, named forum
- Strategic (90-day) → monthly, board or ExCo

**The accountability tone:** Frame monitoring as partnership, not surveillance:
> "To make sure [exec] has what they need, I'll send a weekly update — and flag any blockers immediately."
Not: "I'll be checking in every week to see if this is done."

## Follow-Up Summary Template

Send within 24 hours. Not a long document — one page maximum.

---

**[Meeting name] — [Date]**
**To:** [Attendees]

**Decisions made:**
- [Decision 1 — specific, unambiguous]
- [Decision 2 — if applicable]

**Actions:**

| Action | Owner | Deadline | Monitoring |
|--------|-------|----------|-----------|
| [Action 1] | [Name] | [Date] | [How/when tracked] |
| [Action 2] | [Name] | [Date] | [How/when tracked] |

**Escalation path:** [If action stalls, [name] escalates to [name] by [date]]

**Open items:** [Specific question(s) still to be resolved — with owner and date]

**Next check-in:** [Date, forum]

---

**Tone guidance for the follow-up:**
- Factual, not editorial — capture what was decided, not what Lorian thinks about it
- Credit contributions: "Following [exec name]'s suggestion, the [action] will include [X]"
- Warm but professional — not formal, not casual

## Relationship Acknowledgment Moves

Use at least one of these in every close, regardless of how the meeting went:

| Move | When to use | Example language |
|------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Acknowledge a specific contribution** | Always — someone said something useful | "[Name]'s point about [X] made the recommendation sharper — I'll incorporate that." |
| **Name the difficulty** | When the meeting was hard | "I know this is a difficult conversation — I appreciate the directness. It's more useful than a polite yes." |
| **Express genuine appreciation** | When the exec's input genuinely helped | "That reframe on [X] is actually better than what I came in with — thank you." |
| **Repair move** | When the meeting was tense | "I want to make sure we're good — I know that was a challenging conversation. I'm committed to [specific follow-up]." |

**The rule:** Never leave the room without at least acknowledging the exec's time and input. The relationship is a long-term asset. The decision is today's outcome.

## Outputs

**Preparation mode:**
- Close language prepared for each likely outcome type (full / partial / deferral)
- Action structure ready to fill in during the close
- Relationship acknowledgment moves identified for specific people in the room

**Post-meeting mode:**
- Follow-up summary drafted and ready to send (with placeholder data filled in)
- Relationship notes: any repair or follow-up needed beyond the summary

## Guidelines

- Never leave without naming the specific next step — "we'll follow up" ends the meeting with nothing committed
- The action structure (6 elements) is non-negotiable — responsibility without accountability, or accountability without a deadline, means the action will not happen
- The follow-up must go within 24 hours — after that, memories diverge and commitments erode
- Relationship acknowledgment is not optional or soft — it is part of the close structure
- After preparing the close, ask: "If you left the room right now, would every person in it know exactly what was decided and what happens next? And would they feel good about having been there?"
- Proactively flag: "The most common failure at close is a vague next step. An exec meeting that ends without a committed decision and a named owner has failed — regardless of how good the presentation was."
