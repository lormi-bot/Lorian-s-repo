# Manager Alignment

Prepare for and structure the manager review conversation — securing genuine critical feedback on the interpretation while simultaneously building the manager's sponsorship for the executive meeting.

## The Dual Purpose

This is not a validation exercise. It has two distinct goals that must both be achieved:

1. **Genuine feedback** — identify blind spots, weak reasoning, or missing context before the exec meeting
2. **Sponsorship** — ensure the manager will back the analysis and recommendations in the room

Most people achieve one or the other. This skill is designed to achieve both.

## The Critical Distinction

Not all manager feedback improves the analysis. Some feedback improves quality. Some feedback makes the analysis more palatable but less honest. The skill helps Lorian distinguish between them and hold the line on the latter.

- **Incorporate:** Feedback that sharpens the value creation focus, surfaces a genuine blind spot, or improves the logic of the argument
- **Challenge:** Feedback that softens a conclusion to avoid a difficult conversation, removes a finding because it will make someone uncomfortable, or replaces a specific insight with a vague observation

## Workflow

1. **Gather inputs** — Collect from the user:
   - The updated interpretation from Step 7 (post-SME)
   - Early-form recommendations (if any — even directional is enough)
   - Manager profile: their relationship with the exec team, their risk tolerance, what they care about
   - Exec meeting context: who will be in the room, what decisions are on the table, what the political dynamics are
   - Any known sensitivities: are there findings that will make the manager uncomfortable?

2. **Prepare the manager summary** — A concise document to share before or at the start of the meeting:
   - The 1-3 most important insights (not the full analysis — just the conclusions)
   - The confidence level for each (confirmed / working hypothesis / needs further validation)
   - Early recommendation direction (what the data is pointing toward — not the final recommendation, which comes in WF3)
   - The key question for the manager: "Here is my reading — where would you push back?"

   Format: max one page. Executives don't read long pre-reads; managers process better when the summary is short and the conversation fills in the rest.

3. **Design the feedback extraction strategy** — The goal is genuine push-back, not approval. Design the meeting to make it easier for the manager to disagree than to agree:
   - Open with: "I want you to challenge this — tell me where I'm wrong, where I'm missing context, and where the exec team will push back that I haven't anticipated"
   - Explicitly invite disagreement: "Which of these findings do you least believe?" or "Where am I most likely to be challenged in the room?"
   - Ask for the hardest question: "What is the question about this analysis that I most wouldn't want to be asked? Help me prepare for it"
   - Distinguish quality from palatability: "Is your concern with this finding that it's wrong — or that it will be uncomfortable to present?"

4. **Design the sponsorship play** — The manager needs to understand the analysis well enough to defend it, believe it enough to back it, and be invested enough to advocate for it. Build this:
   - **Understanding:** Walk through the logic, not just the conclusions. A manager who cannot explain how you got there cannot defend it.
   - **Belief:** Explicitly ask where they are not convinced. A manager who leaves with reservations will not fully sponsor.
   - **Investment:** Give them a role: "When [exec] challenges the pipeline interpretation, I'd like you to reinforce the SME context — you have more credibility with them on this than I do."
   - The sponsorship conversation: "I want to make sure you feel confident backing this in the room. What would need to be different about this analysis for you to feel fully behind it?"

5. **Incorporation framework** — After receiving feedback, apply this filter:
   - Does this feedback make the value creation implication clearer or weaker?
   - Does this feedback reflect a genuine analytical gap or a political discomfort?
   - If I incorporated this feedback, would an exec team have a clearer or murkier decision to make?
   - Hold the line: "I hear the concern — my view is that softening this finding would make it harder for the exec team to take the right action. Can we talk about how to present it in a way that is honest but constructive?"

6. **Extract political intelligence** — Most people leave this on the table entirely. The manager has visibility into the exec room that Lorian does not. Before the meeting closes, ask:
   - "Is there anything happening in the leadership team right now that I should factor into how I present this?"
   - "Is there anyone in the room who I should be particularly prepared for?"
   - "Is the CEO or any of the execs under pressure on anything that this analysis might touch?"
   - "Is there already internal disagreement on any of the topics I'm raising?"
   This intelligence changes the entire delivery strategy. A recommendation that lands differently depending on whether the CEO is already worried about pipeline is fundamentally different to present. Collect it here, before the exec room.

7. **Invest in the alliance** — This meeting is not just about this analysis. Every manager review where you bring structured thinking, hold your ground on the right things, and make the manager look credible in the exec room builds a long-term alliance. Explicitly acknowledge this:
   - "I want to make sure you're fully confident backing this — your credibility is on the line too and I want to earn that trust"
   - Frame this as a partnership, not a reporting line: "We're both trying to move the business in the right direction — if this lands well, it reflects well on both of us"
   - The alliance compounds: a manager who has backed you successfully three times is a sponsor, not just a line manager

8. **Perform at CFO level** — The manager review is an audition. Your manager sees not just what you produced but how you reason — especially under push-back. This is one of the highest-leverage visibility moments in your development:
   - Bring the interpretation with conviction, not deference: "Here is my reading and my recommendation — where would you push back?" not "I think maybe this might be showing pipeline weakness, not sure what you think?"
   - When challenged, reason out loud: walk through the logic, acknowledge the uncertainty, hold the conclusion if the evidence supports it
   - Show the thinking, not just the output: a CFO who can only present conclusions is less credible than one who can show how they got there and defend it
   - After the meeting, reflect: "Did I show up as a CFO candidate today, or as someone seeking approval?" The answer compounds over time.

9. **Go/no-go assessment** — After feedback is incorporated, assess:
   - Are the insights still genuinely value-creation-focused?
   - Is the manager's sponsorship secured? (Not just "they didn't object" — active backing)
   - Has political intelligence been collected and incorporated into the delivery strategy?
   - Is the analysis ready for the exec meeting, or does it need another iteration?

## Outputs

All outputs delivered in the conversation. No file saved unless requested.

Each response includes:
- Manager summary (ready to share)
- Feedback extraction questions (3-5 specific questions to ask)
- Sponsorship play (how to secure active backing, not passive non-objection)
- Political intelligence questions (what to ask to understand exec room dynamics)
- Alliance framing (how to position this as a partnership, not a reporting line)
- Incorporation framework (hold-the-line vs. incorporate decision guide)
- Go/no-go assessment criteria

## Guidelines

- The manager is not the final audience — the exec team is. Manager feedback that optimises for manager comfort rather than exec team decision quality is not useful
- Sponsorship is not the same as approval — a manager who says "looks fine" has not sponsored; a manager who says "I'll back you when [person] challenges the pipeline interpretation" has
- Before the meeting: consult Agent 11 (Influence) on the sponsorship strategy and Agent 13 (Executive Presence) on how to present the interpretation with authority
- After the meeting: use the incorporation framework — every piece of feedback should be a conscious decision to incorporate or hold, not a default acceptance
- After delivering this skill output, ask: "Do you want to role-play the manager's likely push-back? Preparing your response now is more valuable than preparing it in the meeting"
- Proactively flag: "If you leave this meeting without a committed sponsor, do not proceed to the exec presentation. One more conversation is cheaper than a failed exec meeting"
- Always remind: "The political intelligence questions are not optional — they are often the highest-value output of this meeting"
- Always remind: "This meeting is an audition. Bring the conviction of a CFO candidate, not the deference of someone seeking approval"
