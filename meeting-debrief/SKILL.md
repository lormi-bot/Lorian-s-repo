# Meeting Debrief

Convert every executive meeting into specific capability development — not just a result.

> **v1 — Baseline.** This skill compounds over time. Every debrief builds on the last. Update exec personas after each session.

## The Purpose

Most people debrief meetings based on outcome: "we got the decision" or "they weren't ready." This confuses luck with skill. A good outcome can happen despite weak delivery; a poor outcome can happen despite strong delivery. The debrief must focus on **capability and behaviour** — independent of whether the decision went the right way.

Lorian's identified defaults (direct push-back, silence, internal frustration that leaks non-verbally) will not change without deliberate reflection. This skill structures that reflection.

## Run This Debrief Within 2 Hours of the Meeting

Memory is sharpest immediately after. The emotional charge has to settle enough to reflect honestly — but not so long that the specific moments are forgotten.

## Workflow

### Step 1 — Outcome Assessment

Start with a clean honest assessment — not rationalisation.

Ask:
- What was the outcome? Full decision / partial decision / deferral / no progress
- Was this the best possible outcome given the starting conditions — or did delivery leave value on the table?
- If deferral or no progress: what specifically drove that — content, delivery, political dynamics, or something else?

**The honest test:** If the outcome was good, can Lorian explain specifically why — in terms of what they did? If the outcome was poor, can Lorian explain it without attributing it entirely to "they weren't ready" or "the politics"?

---

### Step 2 — Default Pattern Analysis

This is the most important step. Name what happened, trace the trigger, demonstrate the alternative.

**For each moment where a default appeared:**

1. **Name it:** "At the point where [exec] said [X], I [pushed back directly / went quiet / felt the frustration internally and it showed]."

2. **Trace the trigger:** What set it off?
   - Was it the content of what they said (data challenge, political deflection, blame)?
   - Was it how they said it (dismissive tone, interruption, disrespect)?
   - Was it what it meant about Lorian (challenge to credibility, threat to the recommendation)?

3. **Identify the underlying SCARF dimension or EQ trigger:**
   - Status threatened? Certainty lost? Autonomy removed?
   - Internal frustration activated before external response?

4. **Demonstrate the better move:** Not "be more curious" — but the specific sentence:
   > "When [exec] said [exact words], the better response was: '[specific language from the reaction handler]'"

5. **Identify the humor/warmth opportunity that was missed:**
   > "Before responding to that challenge, there was a natural moment for [specific light move]. That would have broken the tension before the argument."

---

### Step 3 — Wins to Reinforce

Equally important: name what worked, specifically. Capability only compounds when effective moves are consciously identified and repeated.

Ask:
- What was the single most effective move in the whole meeting?
- Was there a moment where the Third Option was used instead of a default — and it worked?
- Did humor or warmth land well? When?
- Was there a moment where curiosity disarmed something that push-back would have escalated?

**Format:** "When [exec] said [X], I [did Y], and it worked because [Z]. I want to consciously repeat this."

---

### Step 4 — Gaps to Develop

Identify the specific capability gap — not a vague "I need to handle objections better" but a precise development target.

**Good gap statements:**
- "When I get a political deflection ('that's Sales' problem'), I default to agreeing it's Sales' problem instead of redirecting to the system. I need to practise the system reframe move."
- "I need to be better at reading silence — I fill it immediately, which signals anxiety. The move is: let it breathe for 4-5 seconds and then ask an open question."
- "I missed the warmth opportunity when the CEO made the offhand comment about the Q3 results. That was a natural shared-absurdity moment and I treated it as a data point."

**For each gap:** Identify the specific language to practise. If a skill can be used to practise it (Exec Simulator), flag it.

---

### Step 5 — Updated Exec Personas

What was learned about each person in the room that updates the profile?

For each exec:
- What was their actual objection type in this meeting — confirmed or corrected from the WF4 persona?
- What landed well with them (what moved them)?
- What created resistance (what triggered them)?
- What is the single most useful thing to know about them for the next meeting?

**Format:** Brief notes to update the exec persona file — not a full rewrite, just the new intelligence.

---

### Step 6 — Single Highest-Leverage Development Focus

Out of everything identified in this debrief, what single capability development would have the biggest impact on the next meeting?

**Not a list — one specific thing:**
> "Before the next meeting, practise [specific move] using the Exec Simulator with [specific scenario]. The goal is to have [specific language] feel natural rather than rehearsed."

---

### Step 7 — Feedback to WF3/WF4

Does anything from this meeting suggest changes to how recommendations are shaped or presented in future?

- Did an objection appear that wasn't in the WF3 objection map? → Add it
- Did a persona behave differently than expected? → Update the persona profile
- Was the Safety section missing something that got raised in the room? → Flag for next deck
- Did the narrative structure need to be adapted in the room? → Inform WF4 narrative design for next time

---

## Outputs

Delivered in the conversation immediately after the meeting.

- Outcome assessment (honest, not rationalised)
- Default pattern analysis: each moment identified, triggered, and demonstrated with better language
- Wins: specific effective moves to consciously repeat
- Gaps: precise development targets with specific language to practise
- Updated exec persona notes
- Single highest-leverage development focus (one specific thing)
- WF3/WF4 feedback flags (anything to update in next cycle)

## Guidelines

- Run within 2 hours — memory is sharpest and the emotional charge has settled enough to reflect honestly
- Default patterns must be named explicitly — not "the objection was hard to handle" but "I pushed back directly and it triggered defensiveness"
- Wins are as important as gaps — capability compounds only when effective moves are identified and consciously repeated
- The single development focus must be specific enough to practise — not a principle but a move
- After the debrief, ask: "Is there anyone in that room you still don't feel prepared for? Run the Exec Simulator with them before the next meeting."
- Proactively flag: "The goal of the debrief is not to feel good or bad about the outcome — it is to take one specific thing away that makes the next meeting better. If you can't name it, the debrief hasn't finished."
- Consult Agents 10 (EQ), 11 (Influence), 12 (Conversational Intelligence), and 13 (Executive Presence) for the deepest coaching on specific moments from the meeting
