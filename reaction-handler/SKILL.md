# Reaction Handler

Diagnose exec reactions in real time — and respond with the right move, the right language, and the right navigation toward the decision.

> **v1 — Baseline.** Upgrade when Executive Buy-in Presentation System course PDF arrives (29 March 2026). Enrich after every real meeting.

## The Purpose

Step 4 (Read and Handle Reactions) is where value-enhancing decisions are won or lost, and where relationships are preserved or damaged. Most analytical professionals have a repertoire of two moves: argue harder (direct push-back) or say nothing (silence). Both make things worse.

This skill replaces defaults with diagnosis. The right response depends on what the reaction actually is — not what it looks like on the surface.

## Two Modes

**Preparation mode** — used before the meeting to work through anticipated reactions
**Debrief mode** — used after the meeting to coach on what actually happened

Ask Lorian which mode at the start.

## Reaction Diagnosis Framework

Before choosing a response, diagnose the reaction:

| Reaction type | What it looks like | What it often really means | Response direction |
|--------------|-------------------|---------------------------|-------------------|
| **Analytical** | "The data doesn't support that" / "I'd read that differently" | Genuine data question, or discomfort with the conclusion expressed analytically | Evidence chain + invite the specific disagreement |
| **Political** | "That would create problems with [colleague]" / deflection to someone else's area | Protecting a relationship, protecting their own exposure, or genuine political concern | Acknowledge the relational concern; reframe as supporting, not targeting, the person |
| **Emotional** | Strong pushback, raised voice, dismissive language | Status threat, identity threat, loss of autonomy, or fatigue | Lower your energy first; curiosity question; do not match the intensity |
| **Tactical** | "Let's take this offline" / "Let's revisit in Q1" / vague deferral | Buying time, avoiding a decision in this forum, protecting optionality | Surface the specific concern driving the deferral; ask what would need to be resolved |
| **Silence** | No response; sceptical expression; looks away | Processing, resistance, disagreement they're not ready to voice, or disengagement | Do not fill it with reassurance; let it breathe; then open question: "What's your reaction to that?" |
| **Blame deflection** | "That's a Sales issue" / "That happened before my time" | Political protection, avoiding accountability, or genuine attribution disagreement | Redirect to the system, not the person: "What in the system made that outcome more likely?" |
| **Surface agreement** | "Yes, interesting" with no commitment | Politeness without engagement; not yet convinced; waiting to see others' reactions | Do not take it as a yes; invite genuine input: "I'd really value your specific reaction — is there anything you'd push back on?" |

## Response Move Library

**Acknowledge without agreeing:**
> "That's an important concern — tell me more about what's driving it."
Use when: the concern has merit but the conclusion from it is wrong; you need more information before responding.

**Invite the specific disagreement:**
> "Let me walk through the evidence chain — and please tell me exactly where you'd read it differently."
Use when: analytical objection; the exec has a different data view; you want to find the specific point of divergence.

**Reframe to the system:**
> "What in the system made that outcome more likely? I want to understand the root, not the symptom."
Use when: blame deflection; the conversation is stuck on who is responsible rather than what to do.

**Reframe to cost of inaction:**
> "The question isn't whether this is easy — it's whether the cost of not acting is higher."
Use when: resource or timing objection; exec is focused on the difficulty of acting rather than the risk of not acting.

**Lower and open:**
> "I can see this lands differently — help me understand your perspective."
Use when: emotional pushback; the exec's intensity has risen; matching it will escalate, not resolve.

**The collaborative hypothesis:**
> "What would need to be true for you to feel comfortable moving forward?"
Use when: stuck; the specific concern is unclear; you want to make the exec a co-architect of the solution.

**The graceful time-buy:**
> "That's exactly the right question — can I come back to it in two minutes when we get to the evidence section?"
Use when: unexpected challenge; you need a moment; the answer is coming in a later slide.

**Name the dynamic:**
> "I sense there's a concern here that goes beyond the analysis — am I reading that right?"
Use when: something unspoken is driving the resistance; the surface objection doesn't explain the energy behind it.

**The warmth move:**
> "Fair point — and I'll be honest, I wrestled with that one too."
Use when: tension is building; a moment of self-deprecating honesty would break it without surrendering ground.

**Hold the line:**
> "I hear the concern, and I want to make sure we address it. My view is that a softer version would make it harder to hit [objective]. Can we talk about how to address [the concern] while keeping the direction intact?"
Use when: pressure to dilute the recommendation; the concern is real but the recommendation should hold.

**The concession bridge:**
> "I'm open to starting with [smaller step] — with a 90-day review built in. Would that make the commitment easier?"
Use when: genuine resistance requires a face-saving path; used after hold-the-line has been stated, never as the first move.

## Navigating to a Decision (Step 5)

As the meeting progresses, actively guide toward the decision without forcing it.

**When consensus is forming — name it:**
> "It sounds like we're aligned that [X] is the priority — is that right?"

**When the room is stuck — surface the sticking point:**
> "It seems like the main question is whether [Y] is a risk worth taking. How should we think about that together?"

**When one person is blocking:**
- Understand the block before trying to overcome it: "What would need to be true for you to support this direction?"
- Is it analytical (they need more evidence), political (they need a face-saving path), or emotional (they need to feel heard)?
- Match the response move to the type

**When time is running out:**
- Partial decision is better than no decision: "We may not resolve everything today — but can we commit to [specific part]? I'll take the open question away and come back by [date]."

**When a decision cannot be reached today:**
- Define the specific question, not just "we need more information"
- Define the specific next step and date: "The question is [X]. I'll have an answer by [date] and we can decide at [forum]."

## Debrief Mode — Coaching Structure

For each moment that went off-track:

1. **What happened:** Describe the exchange — what the exec said, what Lorian said
2. **Reaction diagnosis:** What type was it actually? (May be different from what it looked like)
3. **What triggered the default:** If push-back or silence appeared — what set it off?
4. **The better move:** Which response move would have worked better?
5. **The exact language:** "When they said [X], respond with: [specific sentence]"
6. **The humor/warmth opportunity:** Was there a tension-breaking moment that was missed? What would it have looked like?

## Outputs

**Preparation mode:**
- Anticipated reaction diagnosed by type
- Recommended response move with specific language
- Navigation move toward the decision
- Warmth/humor opportunity if applicable

**Debrief mode:**
- Exchange analysis (what happened, what it meant)
- Default pattern identified if triggered (with trigger analysis)
- Better move demonstrated with exact language
- Missed warmth/humor opportunities named
- One specific thing to do differently next time

## Guidelines

- Always diagnose before prescribing — the wrong response move for the reaction type makes things worse, not better
- The default patterns (direct push-back, silence) must be named explicitly when they appear in debrief — not glossed over
- Humor and warmth are response tools, not rewards for a good meeting — coach on when they were available and missed
- After debrief, ask: "Did you hold the recommendation while preserving the relationship? Both must be true."
- Proactively flag: "The most common failure in Step 4 is responding to what the exec said, not to what they meant. The surface objection is rarely the real one."
- Consult Agents 10 (EQ), 11 (Influence), 12 (Conversational Intelligence), and 13 (Executive Presence) for the deepest coaching on specific moments
